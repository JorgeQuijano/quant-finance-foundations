# Probability Spaces & Random Variables

## Probability Space

A probability space is a triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$:

- $\Omega$ — sample space, the set of all possible outcomes
- $\mathcal{F}$ — sigma-algebra, collection of measurable subsets of $\Omega$
- $\mathbb{P}$ — probability measure, a function that assigns probabilities to events in $\mathcal{F}$

The sigma-algebra $\mathcal{F}$ captures what events we are allowed to reason about. A sigma-algebra must contain $\Omega$, be closed under complement, and closed under countable unions.

## Random Variables

A random variable $X$ is a measurable function $X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$. "Measurable" means for every Borel set $B \subset \mathbb{R}$, the preimage $X^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F}$. This is what makes probability well-defined — we only assign probabilities to events that are in $\mathcal{F}$.

The distribution of $X$ is described by the probability measure $\mathbb{P}_X(A) = \mathbb{P}(X \in A)$.

## Expectation

For a random variable $X$ with distribution $\mathbb{P}_X$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_\Omega X(\omega) \, d\mathbb{P}(\omega) = \int_{\mathbb{R}} x \, d\mathbb{P}_X(x)$$

For discrete distributions: $\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_i x_i \mathbb{P}(X = x_i)$

For continuous distributions with density $f$: $\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f(x) \, dx$

## Variance

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X])^2] = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$$

Standard deviation is $\sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}$.

## Key Distributions in Finance

### Gaussian (Normal) $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$
The workhorse. Used for returns in the Black-Scholes world.
$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$$

Properties:
- Completely described by $\mu$ and $\sigma$
- Linear combinations of normals are normal
- In finance: log-returns are often modeled as normal (prices are lognormal)

### Lognormal
If $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ then $S = e^X$ is lognormal.
Used for stock prices in the Black-Scholes model.

### Student-t
Thicker tails than normal. Useful for modeling returns that have extreme events more often than a Gaussian predicts.
$$f(x) = \frac{\Gamma\left(\frac{\nu+1}{2}\right)}{\sqrt{\nu\pi}\Gamma\left(\frac{\nu}{2}\right)} \left(1 + \frac{x^2}{\nu}\right)^{-\frac{\nu+1}{2}}$$
Degrees of freedom $\nu$: smaller $\nu$ = heavier tails. $\nu \to \infty$ converges to normal.

### Cauchy
Stable distribution with no finite mean. Heavier tails than Student-t. Rarely used directly in practice but appears in theory (e.g., some limit theorems).

## Law of Large Numbers (LLN)

Let $X_1, X_2, ...$ be i.i.d. random variables with finite mean $\mu = \mathbb{E}[X_i]$.

**Weak LLN:** $\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \xrightarrow{p} \mu$ (convergence in probability)

**Strong LLN:** $\bar{X}_n \to \mu$ almost surely (pointwise convergence except on a set of probability zero)

The LLN is the formal reason why averaging works — sample means converge to the true mean as you get more data.

## Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

Let $X_1, ..., X_n$ be i.i.d. with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2 < \infty$.

$$\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)$$

(Convergence in distribution to a standard normal.)

This is the most important theorem in statistics. It says: no matter what the underlying distribution looks like, the *distribution of the sample mean* approaches normal as sample size grows. This is why normality is assumed everywhere in finance — even when it's wrong, CLT-based approximations often work well enough.

## Conditional Expectation

$\mathbb{E}[X | \mathcal{G}]$ is the expected value of $X$ given the information in sigma-algebra $\mathcal{G}$.

If $\mathcal{G} = \sigma(Y)$ (the sigma-algebra generated by random variable $Y$), then $\mathbb{E}[X | Y]$ is a function of $Y$. This is the regression function — it is the best predictor of $X$ given $Y$ in the $L^2$ sense.

**Tower property:** $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X | \mathcal{G}]] = \mathbb{E}[X]$
**Independence:** If $X$ independent of $\mathcal{G}$, then $\mathbb{E}[X | \mathcal{G}] = \mathbb{E}[X]$
